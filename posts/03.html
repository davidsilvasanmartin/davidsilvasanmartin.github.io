Let \(\Omega\) be a set.

Def 1. A \(\sigma\)-field is a collection \(\mathcal{F}\) of subsets of \(\Omega\) such that \(\emptyset \in \mathcal{F}\), and it's closed under complementation and under countable unions.

Def 2. The sets that are members of \(\mathcal{F}\) are said to be measurable with respect to \(\mathcal{F}\), or just measurable if the \(\sigma\)-field is understood from the context.

Def 3. A probability measure \(P\) on a \(\sigma\)-field \(\mathcal{F}\) of subsets of \(\Omega\) is a function: \begin{align} P: \ &\mathcal{F}\longrightarrow \left[0, 1\right] \\ &A \mapsto P(A) \end{align} such that:

  1. \( P(\Omega) = 1 \).
  2. For each pairwise disjoint sequence of elements of \(\mathcal{F}, A_m, m \in \mathbb{N}:\) \(P(\cup_{i=1}^\infty A_i) = \sum_{i=1}^\infty P(A_i)\).

For each \(A \in \mathcal{F}\), \(P(A)\) is called the probability of \(A\).

Def 4. A probability space is a triple \((\Omega, \mathcal{F}, P)\) where

  • \(\Omega\) is a set.
  • \(\mathcal{F}\) is a \(\sigma\)-field of subsets of \(\Omega\).
  • \(P\) is a probability measure on \(\mathcal{F}\).

\(\Omega\) is called the sample space and its members are called sample points. The members of \(\mathcal{F}\) are called events. We sometimes say that \(P\) is a probability measure on \(\Omega\) or on \((\Omega, \mathcal{F})\) rather than on \(\mathcal{F}\).

Notation. We will often not distinguish between the sample point \(\omega\) and the event \(\{\omega\}\).

Def 5. If \(\Omega\) is a topological space, the Borel \(\sigma\)-field of \(\Omega\) is the one generated by the collection of all open sets. The members of the Borel \(\sigma\)-field are called Borel sets.

Notation. We will denote the Borel \(\sigma\)-field of \(\mathbb{R}\) by \(\mathbb{B}\).

Def 6. A measurable space is a pair \((\Psi, \mathcal{G})\) where \(\Psi\) is a nonempty set and \(\mathcal{G}\) is a \(\sigma\)-field of subsets of \(\Psi\).

Remark. If \((\Omega, \mathcal{F}, P)\) si a probability space, then \((\Omega, \mathcal{F})\) is a measurable space.

Def 7. Let \((\Omega, \mathcal{F})\) and \((\Psi, \mathcal{G})\) be measurable spaces. A measurable function from \((\Omega, \mathcal{F})\) to \((\Psi, \mathcal{G})\) is a function \(X: \Omega \rightarrow \Psi\) such that \(X^{-1}(B) \in \mathcal{F}\) for all \(B \in \mathcal{G}\).

Def 8. A random variable defined on a probability space \((\Omega, \mathcal{F}, P)\) is a measurable function from \((\Omega, \mathcal{F})\) to \((\mathbb{R}, \mathbb{B})\). As a consequence, \(X^{-1}(B) \in \mathcal{F}\) for each \(B \in \mathbb{B}\).

Notation. For \(B \in \mathbb{B}\), the following expressions are equivalent:

\begin{align} X^{-1}(B) &\equiv \{ \omega \in \Omega \mid X(\omega) \in B\} \equiv \{X \in B\};\\ P \{ X \in B \} &\equiv P \{\omega \in \Omega \mid X(\omega) \in B\} \end{align}

Prop 1. If \(X\) is a random variable on \((\Omega, \mathcal{F}, P)\) and the function \(g: \mathbb{R} \rightarrow \mathbb{R}\) is measurable respect to \(\mathbb{B}\), then

\begin{align} g(X): \ &\Omega \longrightarrow &\mathbb{R} \longrightarrow &\mathbb{R} \\ &A \mapsto &X(A) \mapsto &g(X(A)) \end{align}

is a random variable. In particular, this conclusion is true when \(g\) is a continuous function.
