Each man then randomly selects one. Find the expected number of men who select their own hats.\\ \textit{Commentary on the book's solution:}\\ Let $X$ be the number of men that select their own hats. There are 2 ways the hat selection can be carried out. Let's describe them first.\\ \begin{enumerate} \item \underline{With replacement}. Each of the men selects a hat, but they don't keep it: hats are thrown back immediately into the center of the room. Man 1 has $N$ hats to choose from. So do each of the other men. For the particular case $N=2$, all possible options are shown on the following table: \begin{table}[H] \centering \begin{tabular}{|c|c|c|c|c|} \hline Event & $A_1$ & $A_2$ & $A_3$ & $A_4$\\\hline \makecell{Selected \\ hats} & \makecell{$M1-H1$\\$M2-H1$} & \makecell{$M1-H1$\\$M2-H2$} & \makecell{$M1-H2$\\$M2-H1$} & \makecell{$M1-H2$\\$M2-H2$} \\\hline $P(A_j)$ & $1/4$ & $1/4$ & $1/4$ & $1/4$ \\\hline $X$ & 1 & 2 & 0 & 1 \\\hline \end{tabular} \caption{Hat selection cases, $N=2$. With replacement.} \label{Values} \end{table} Where $Mi-Hj$ means that man $i$ selects the hat $j$, which belongs to $Mj$.\\ \item \underline{Without replacement}. Man 1 selects a hat, and then he keeps it until the round is over. Man 2 will have $N-1$ hats to choose from, man 3 will have $N-2$, and so on. For the particular case $N=2$, the options are: \begin{table}[H] \centering \begin{tabular}{|c|c|c|} \hline & $A_1$ & $A_2$ \\\hline \makecell{Selected \\ hats} & \makecell{$M1-H1$\\$M2-H2$} & \makecell{$M1-H2$\\$M2-H1$} \\\hline $P(A_j)$ & $1/2$ & $1/2$ \\\hline $X$ & 2 & 0 \\\hline \end{tabular} \caption{Hat selection cases, $N=2$. Without replacement.} \label{Values} \end{table} The result that $$P\{ i \text{th man selects his own hat} \} = 1/N, \text{ for all } i \text{ and } N,$$ holds for either of the 2 cases. This is easily seen by counting the cases in which man 1 selects hat 1 and adding up probabilities, and doing the same for man 2. We won't provide a formal proof here.\\ \end{enumerate} \textbf{Exercise 3.14. [The matching rounds problem]} Suppose in Exercise 2.30 that those choosing their own hats depart, while the others (those without a match) leave all other hats in the center of the room, mix them up, and then re-select.
